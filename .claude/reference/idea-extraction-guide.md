# Idea Extraction Guide

Use this whenever generating or rewriting idea cards for Clouds.
Apply every rule below before writing a single word.

---

## Card structure

```json
{
  "id": "NNN",
  "hook": "The opening line. ≤12 words.",
  "angles": [
    "Angle 01: contrarian snapback — context lean + scroll stop + snap to opposite. State question planted.",
    "Angle 02: pain or benefit lead — open with what they want or fear, then reveal.",
    "Angle 03: staccato take, story, or observation — concrete, short sentences, visual direction."
  ],
  "close": "1-sentence payoff that resolves the curiosity loop opened by the hook. Only earned at the end of the video.",
  "category": "time | identity | system | money | fear | design | creativity | environment",
  "status": "unused"
}
```

## Energy and tone

This content library is for a life design creator. The energy is expansive, permission-giving, and curious. Not trapped.

**The right energy:**
- Life can be designed. Start there.
- "You do not need permission to begin. You never did."
- "Most walls are local." They dissolve when tested.
- Courage, perspective shifts, creative possibility, identity fluidity.
- The observer who sees what others have normalized and names it.

**The wrong energy:**
- Sunday dread, corporate-trapped anxiety, job-escape fantasy.
- Hustle culture critique (it's been done; it's not the voice).
- Generic motivational arcs with a tidy resolution.

**Tone by category:**
- `environment`: somatic, observational, specific cities and geographies.
- `identity`: diagnostic of inherited schemas, not self-help cheerleading.
- `design`: iterative, version-based, no final answer energy.
- `creativity`: permission-giving, fear-as-filter, making as research.
- `fear`: reframe the accounting, not the emotion.
- `system`: architectural, not motivational.
- `time`: mathematical, concrete, non-refundable.
- `money`: sufficiency over wealth, autonomy as the actual target.

## Curiosity loop (Zeigarnik Effect)

Every card opens a loop in the hook that only closes at the end of the video.

**How it works:**
- The hook plants a single question the viewer cannot answer without watching.
- The angles develop the middle — the question stays open.
- The `close` field is the 1-sentence payoff. One clean resolution. Never mid-video.

**Writing the close:**
- It should feel like the thing the whole video was building toward.
- It is not a summary. It is a revelation or a reframe.
- It should make the hook retroactively more interesting.
- ≤20 words. One clean sentence.

**Example:**
- Hook: "You have four thousand weeks. That's the whole number."
- Close: "The number was always the number. Making it visible is just finally agreeing to know it."

## Hook rules

A hook is not a sentence. It's a moment. Three elements make it work together: the words you say, the visuals on screen, and the pacing of the first few seconds. Get all three right and the viewer stops scrolling. Miss any one and you lose them.

The card captures the words (the spoken hook field). The angles carry the visual direction and pacing notes. When filming, the moment is built by combining the card's hook line with the chosen angle's visual treatment.

### The single subject, single question test

After writing any hook, run these two checks:

1. **Subject check:** Is it possible for someone to misunderstand what this video is about? If yes, rewrite. The subject must be unmistakably clear before you move on.

2. **Question check:** What single question pops in the viewer's mind after hearing this hook? It should be one question, and it should be shock-inducing: "How is this possible? Is this real? Can you tell me more? I can't believe that." If you can't immediately name the single question, rewrite.

If 50,000 people watch your hook, all 50,000 should be curious about the same thing. Fragmented curiosity = fragmented attention = churn.

### The three-step hook formula

This is the gold standard structure. Use it as Angle 01 on every card.

**Step 1 — Context lean-in:** First line establishes the topic clearly AND gets the viewer leaning in. Do this by establishing common ground, referencing a benefit or pain point they already have, or saying something surprising. They self-select in or out here.

**Step 2 — Scroll stop interjection:** A single line that stuns. Uses a contrasting word: "But," "But here's the thing," "But it turns out," "But here's what's interesting." This is the setup. It stops momentum and signals that a reversal is coming.

**Step 3 — Contrarian snapback:** Goes in the opposite direction of the initial lean. Still on-topic but snaps the viewer to a different path. The bigger the shock, the better the snap. This is the haymaker that plants the question.

Example:
> "The tech in the Vegas Sphere is insane. Biggest screen ever built, 20 times bigger than an IMAX." (context lean)
> "But get this. The screen is actually the least impressive part." (scroll stop)
> "Because the most impressive part is the audio. This is going to blow your mind." (contrarian snapback)

Question planted: how can the audio be more impressive than the biggest screen ever built?

### Hook types

**1. Strong take** — assert a position that forces agreement or argument.
> "You're not lazy. The ceiling is just too low."
> "The financially safe choice is usually the most fragile structure."

**2. Small epiphany** — name something people have felt but never articulated.
> "Sunday night isn't anxiety. It's when the math becomes impossible to avoid."
> "Your nervous system has a cost of living too."

**3. Story starter** — drop the listener into a specific scenario mid-scene.
> "Describe an average Tuesday. Five years from now. Nothing changed."

**4. Emotional opener** — lead with a vulnerable or exposing line. Works because it's the opposite of what people expect on social media. Specificity matters more than intensity.
> "I cried over my own content this morning."
> "I left the country at 19. I told no one for three weeks."

**5. Curiosity spike** — name something specific and weird happening right now. Forces the question: wait, what?
> "Something quiet is happening to people who keep moving every two years."
> "There's a thing the most interesting people I know all do. No one talks about it."

What a hook is NOT:
- A listicle ("3 reasons why...")
- Generic advice framing ("Here's how to be more productive")
- Motivational statement ("You have what it takes")
- Multiple subjects competing for attention

### Hook sentence structure: staccato

For the hook especially, use short punchy sentences. Shorter sentences force maximum clarity and increase the density of value per word. Go shorter at the top, then expand to medium and longer sentences as the video continues.

Bad: "There are many different approaches people take when thinking about career transitions and the factors that influence them."
Good: "You know you're good here. You don't know if you're good."

Length: ≤12 words for the hook line itself. Two sentences max. Second sentence is a short punch (4-8 words).

### Lead with benefit or pain, not feature

Instead of: "You should understand geographic arbitrage because it's a financial optimization."
Use: "If you want to think more clearly and feel less pressure, there's one variable most people haven't touched."

Always ask: what does the viewer want, or what are they afraid of? Start there.

### Hook tactics

**Drama test.** Apply to every hook: is this dramatic enough? Could it be more specific, more exposing, more tense? "Here are 3 tips to grow on Instagram" fails. "Here's the reason nobody cares about your Instagram posts" passes. The more dramatic, the harder it is to scroll past.

**Pause for effect.** A pause inside the hook can work, but only deliberately. "I got ghosted ... like hundreds of times" lands harder than the same line without the pause. The pause IS part of the moment. Note where pauses go in the angle direction.

**Word-by-word captions.** Text appearing on screen as the words are spoken keeps the viewer reading along, which holds attention longer than passive listening. Default for every card.

**Highlight the right word visually.** When 3-5 bold words appear on screen as the text hook, they should isolate the noun that creates the curiosity. "Instagram is constantly changing" → highlight INSTAGRAM, not CHANGING. Always ask: which word, alone on screen, makes someone stop scrolling?

**Dynamic opener.** Open with movement, a unique angle, or B-roll → voiceover → talking head. Anything other than "person sitting still, starts talking." The visual shift in the first 2 seconds is half the hook.

**Foreshadow the punchline.** Frontload the most visual or exciting moment of the video as the opener, even if it comes from the middle or end. The rest of the video earns it back.

**Avoid the millennial pause.** Don't take a breath before starting. Trust the camera. The hook starts on frame one.

**Reshoot, don't repurpose.** Long-form openers almost never work as short-form hooks. Film a new hook for every short.

### Hook anti-patterns

Do not write hooks that are:
- Listicles ("3 reasons why...", "Here are 5 tips...")
- Generic how-to framing ("Here's how to grow on Instagram")
- Stat-leads ("80% of creators..." — nobody cares about a stat)
- Motivational platitudes ("You have what it takes")
- Slow openers with no energy or tension
- Carrying multiple subjects competing for attention
- The kind of line you'd get from clipping the start of a longer video

## Angle rules

Each card has 3 angles. An angle is a 2-3 sentence direction for HOW to turn the seed idea into a video. The 3 angles on every card are **3 different content formulas** applied to the same idea — not three variations of the same approach. Each angle should produce a meaningfully different video.

Pick 3 of the 7 formulas below that genuinely fit the seed idea. Not every formula fits every idea. The right 3 depend on which structures the idea can actually support.

### The 7 content formulas

**1. Strong take**
Assert a position and defend it for the whole video. Force agreement or disagreement. Can pivot to education — the take is the hook, then teach the mechanism behind it. Works when the idea has a sharp contrarian edge.

Format: "Strong take: [the position]. [How to defend it or what mechanism to teach]."

**2. Small epiphany**
Name something people have felt but never articulated. The reveal isn't shocking, it's recognizable. Creates instant kinship: "you noticed that too?" Works when the idea names a quiet, unspoken truth.

Format: "Small epiphany: [the felt-but-unnamed thing]. Then unpack why it works that way."

**3. Story**
A specific scene with a turn. Real, concrete, sensory. Drop the viewer into the moment, not the lesson. The lesson is what remains after the story ends. Works when the idea has lived experience behind it.

Format: "Story: tell the moment when [specific scene]. Turn: [what shifted]. Lesson lands implicitly."

**4. Niche-niche playbook**
Narrow an existing topic into one specialty audience's playbook. The general topic is everywhere; the niche-niche is the specific moves for the specific people. Works when the idea has obvious sub-audiences who need it more than the average viewer.

Format: "Niche-niche playbook: [topic] specifically for [narrow audience]. Walk through the 3-5 moves that apply to them."

**5. Would / wouldn't expertise**
"Here's what I would and wouldn't do in [specific situation]." Curated judgment, not advice. Works when the idea is something you've thought about enough to have clear preferences and a clear inverse.

Format: "Would/wouldn't: in [situation], here's what I would do. Here's what I wouldn't. Why."

**6. Curation**
Compile what's happening in a space. You're not the expert, you're the one paying attention. Save the viewer time. Works when the idea is observational and culture-aware.

Format: "Curation: here are the [N] things happening in [space] right now. What they share. What it signals."

**7. Free work**
Do the work publicly as the content. Not "here's how to do X" — actually do X on camera. The doing IS the video. Works when the idea has a tangible output (a design, a teardown, a redesign, a build).

Format: "Free work: actually [do the thing] on camera. The output is the content."

### Picking 3 formulas per card

Aim for variety across the three. Avoid:
- Three formulas that produce similar videos (strong take + would/wouldn't + small epiphany can all become "Valentin asserts opinions")
- Forcing a formula that doesn't fit (a deeply personal observation rarely works as curation)

Good mix patterns:
- One assertive (strong take / would-wouldn't) + one emotional (small epiphany / story) + one observational (curation / niche-niche / free work)
- One that opens the loop hardest + one that closes deepest + one that travels widest

### Visual direction

For each angle, include a one-line visual hint where the formula allows. What's on screen in the first 2 seconds. This anchors the angle to a real shoot.

### Hook techniques still apply

The hook rules section governs HOW you write the opening line within any formula. A "strong take" angle still benefits from the three-step formula. A "story" angle still uses staccato sentence structure. The formula is the video type. The hook techniques are how you open it.

## Three-hook alignment

Every video has three hook components that must reinforce the same single question:
- **Spoken hook** — what you say verbally (the card's `hook` field)
- **Visual hook** — what appears on screen in the first 2 seconds (described in the angle's visual direction)
- **Text hook** — 3-5 bold words displayed on screen, highlighting the curiosity-driving noun

If any two of the three say different things, viewers get confused. Confusion causes churn.

**Picking the text hook words:** isolate the noun that creates the curiosity. "Instagram is constantly changing" → highlight INSTAGRAM. "Your city is writing your identity" → highlight CITY or IDENTITY, not WRITING. Always ask: which word, alone on screen, makes someone stop scrolling?

When writing angles, note the visual direction and the text-hook highlight so the creator can align all three.

## What content actually works (from source transcripts)

**Source: "The Art of Yapping" (May 2025)**

Formats that consistently perform:
- **Strong take**: assert a position. Force disagreement or agreement.
- **Strong take into education**: the take is just the hook. Pivot immediately to teaching something real.
- **Small epiphany**: relatable daily observations people hadn't named. Creates kinship.
- **Story time**: narrative arc, something that actually happened.

What doesn't work:
- "My top 3 favorite X" — listicles
- "3 ways to do Y" — generic how-to
- Motivational speech format
- Anything without a narrative arc or strong opinion

**Source: "Four Hook Mistakes" (May 2025)**

The four hook mistakes:
1. No single subject, no single question — too vague, too many curiosity paths
2. Three-hook misalignment — spoken, visual, and text say different things
3. Visuals don't scroll-stop — not visually differentiated enough to make the viewer lift their thumb
4. Not studying proven data — writing new hooks from scratch every time instead of templating from what worked

**Source: "Six Tips for Hooks" (May 2025)**

Key principles:
- The three-step formula is the gold standard for hook construction
- Visual hooks are 100x more powerful than spoken alone. Always use title text on screen.
- Speed to value: frontload the best stuff. Short form has a 4-second window before churn.
- Cult hopping: wrap unknown ideas in known references (celebrities, brands, cultural touchpoints). Creates comfort and common ground subconsciously.
- Staccato sentences in the hook increase density of value per word.

The underlying principle from all sources: **people stay for narrative arcs and strong opinions.** Everything else is filler.

**Source: "The Art of the Personal Brand" — Owen Meets World (2025-05)**

Content comes in distinct formulas, not just hook variations. The 7 that work: strong take, small epiphany, story, niche-niche playbook, would/wouldn't expertise, curation, free work. Each produces a meaningfully different video from the same seed. (This shapes the angle structure above.)

The pillar system: 4 recurring strategic content themes, rotated and tracked monthly. The library should have variety across pillars and across formulas, not just topic categories.

Content market fit = niche-maxing + content TAM. Not every card should aim for broad reach. Some are niche-deep — 1000 right people beat 100k wrong ones.

Positioning strategies that work:
- Counter-position — opposite of what's popular in the niche (low-fi when others are produced, hard when others say easy)
- Mirror with a twist — copy the format, change one production element radically
- Standout demographic — same content, new audience identity

Tribal affinity: wrap unknown ideas in known references (brands, cultural touchpoints, recognizable subcultures). Signal to a tribe that you see them.

The "no competition" reality: 80% never start, 10% quit early, 5% get shiny object syndrome. Three out of 100 finish. The library exists to make consistent showing-up easier, not to chase any one viral hit.

Memorable > perfect. View quality > view quantity.

**Source: "Hooks as Moments" — Jade Bonacolta (2025-05)**

Hooks are no longer single catchy sentences. They are full moments: words + visuals + pacing, executed together in the first few seconds. Miss any one element and the hook fails.

Words: pass the drama test. Specific, surprising, tension-creating. Emotional or exposing openings outperform informational ones. Lead with vulnerability or a curiosity spike, not with a stat or "3 tips."

Visuals: the most under-leveraged element. Movement, dynamic editing, unique angles, word-by-word captions, B-roll into talking-head, foreshadowing the punchline. Static talking head = scroll.

Pacing: bring energy from frame one. Pauses are allowed but only for deliberate effect. The millennial pause (the breath before starting) is a tell that you don't trust your hook.

Text-hook word choice matters: highlight the curiosity-driving noun (CITY, IDENTITY, INSTAGRAM), not the generic verb (writing, changing). The right word isolated on screen is half the hook.

Hooks deserve more reps. Film 3 takes minimum. The third is usually the best. Long-form clips rarely work as hooks for shorts — reshoot.

## Voice DNA rules (always apply)

From: `~/.claude/reference/voice-dna-v1.md`

**Archetype**: The Analyst. Diagnostic, not motivational. Translate the mechanism. Don't cheerlead.

**Cadence**: High variance. Long explanatory sentences (20-30 words) followed by short declarative punches (4-8 words). Short always follows long.

**Order**: Negative diagnosis first, then direction. End with a hook or open question, never a summary.

**Banned vocabulary**: grind, level up, game changer, disrupt, unlock, manifest, abundance, mindset shift, high-value, alpha, sigma, bro.

**AI tells to suppress**: delve, navigate, testament to, in the realm of, a tapestry of, it's worth noting, at its core, the landscape of, underscores, multifaceted, robust, holistic, leverage, pivotal, foster, harness, embark, unpack, "in today's fast-paced world."

**Never use**: em dashes, semicolons, three-part balanced conclusions, neat summative paragraphs, uniform paragraph lengths, pushy/sales language, specific outcome promises.

**Convert**: "I think" / "I believe" to direct assertion or "The truth is."

## Anti-slop checklist

Before finalizing any card, scan against these patterns:

- [ ] No em dashes anywhere in hook or angles
- [ ] Hook passes the single subject test (no ambiguity about what the video is about)
- [ ] Hook passes the single question test (one clear, shock-inducing question is planted)
- [ ] Hook passes the drama test (specific, surprising, tension-creating)
- [ ] Hook uses staccato structure (short sentences, high density)
- [ ] No listicles, stat-leads, generic how-to framing, or motivational platitudes
- [ ] At least one angle includes visual direction for the hook moment
- [ ] Text-hook word identified (the curiosity-driving noun, not a generic verb)
- [ ] Each angle uses a different content formula from the 7 (strong take, small epiphany, story, niche-niche, would/wouldn't, curation, free work)
- [ ] The 3 formulas on this card produce meaningfully different videos (not three flavors of "assert an opinion")
- [ ] Angles include a visual direction hint where the formula allows
- [ ] No formula was forced onto an idea that doesn't support it
- [ ] No banned vocabulary
- [ ] No AI tells
- [ ] No "here's how to" framing in hooks
- [ ] No three-part balanced conclusions
- [ ] Close field present and resolves the hook's curiosity loop (not a summary)
- [ ] Close is ≤20 words, one clean sentence
