# Hook Intelligence Reference

This is the hook-writing knowledge base for the Clouds idea library.
Apply it whenever generating `angles` entries. Every angle contains a spoken hook
and a text hook. Read this before writing either.

---

## The single most important rule

**A hook is a question the idea answers. Not the idea itself.**

If someone reads the spoken hook and already knows the answer — rewrite it.
That is a thesis statement wearing a hook's clothes. It closes the loop instead
of opening it. It gives the content away before the content starts.

### Closed loop vs open loop — before/after pairs

The left column is what bad hook generation produces. The right column is what
the hook should be.

| Closed (wrong) | Open (right) |
|---|---|
| "You don't build discipline. Obsession builds it for you." | "The reason your discipline keeps failing isn't what you think." |
| "Transformation is subtractive, not additive." | "Every habit you've ever built on top of a broken identity collapsed for the same reason." |
| "Vision boards don't move people. The anti-vision does." | "There's a reason your vision board has never moved you. It's not because you lack ambition." |
| "Lock-in is a comeback protocol, not maintenance." | "The most locked-in people I've seen didn't push harder after they fell." |
| "Niche down is industrial-age programming." | "The advice holding your personal brand back came from an era before you could be your own distribution channel." |
| "The identity layer is the prerequisite for any habit change." | "The system isn't the problem. Something underneath the system keeps breaking it." |
| "Obsession creates discipline. Discipline cannot create obsession." | "You've been trying to build the output before the input exists." |

**Pattern in every good hook above:** it names the problem or contradiction
without naming the answer. The reader feels a gap. They need to keep going.

**Pattern in every bad hook above:** it delivers the insight in one line. The
card has nothing left to say that the hook hasn't already said. The reader
has no reason to continue.

### The fastest diagnostic

After writing a spoken hook, ask: "Can a reasonably intelligent person guess
what the card is about to say?"

If yes — the hook is closed. Rewrite.
If no — the hook is open. Proceed.

## What a hook pair is

Each angle produces two hooks that are designed to work together:

**Spoken hook** — A line that works when read aloud. Shorter. Direct address or
bold claim. Said in one breath. The viewer hears it before they read anything.

**Text hook** — Appears on screen or as the opening line of a post. Can be
slightly longer. Uses open-loop punctuation where appropriate. Pairs visually
with the spoken hook to compound the information gap.

They do not say the same thing. The spoken hook opens the loop. The text hook
deepens it or reframes it. Together they create a double-layer of tension.

## The psychology this app's content activates

This content targets the 20-35 audience who has already tried the standard
advice. They are not beginners. They have read the books, done the habits, built
the routines. They are stuck in a different way: the surface behavior changed,
the identity layer did not. That is the specific gap this content operates in.

**System 1 first.** Every hook must register before the reader consciously
processes it. Emotional resonance, pattern interruption, and identity signals
activate System 1. Logic and instruction do not. If the hook sounds like advice,
it is dead on arrival for this audience.

**The information gap must be calibrated.** Too wide = the reader feels lost.
Too narrow = they feel they already know and disengage. This audience is
well-read. The gap works when they have *enough context to care but not enough to
resolve it.* The sweet spot: they have a hunch that the content will confirm or
destroy something they already suspect.

**Identity threat outperforms curiosity for this niche.** The reader self-concept
is: "I am someone who thinks seriously about how I operate." A hook that
challenges that self-image produces more pull than a hook that promises
information. Lead with the diagnosis that implies the reader's self-model is
wrong before offering the alternative.

## The trigger stack

Every hook should contain at least two layers. The strongest contain three.

**Layer 1 — Interrupt.** Break the pattern of the feed. Violated expectation,
counterintuitive claim, or a statement that belongs to a different conversation.
Forces: "wait, what?"

**Layer 2 — Gap.** Create an unresolved question or open loop. The reader must
keep going to close it. Never close the loop in the hook itself.

**Layer 3 — Emotionalize.** Attach a feeling to the cognitive interest. Loss
aversion, identity threat, or validation of a secret suspicion. The reader now
*needs* to know, not just *wants* to.

Optional **Layer 4 — Legitimize.** Specificity, authority, or social proof that
quiets skepticism before it can override engagement. Works best when the claim
is bold enough to trigger doubt.

## The ten most effective patterns for this content

### 1. The Causal Inversion

State the thing the audience is doing, then flip the causal arrow.

> "You're not stuck because you lack discipline. You lack discipline because you're
> stuck on the wrong thing."

Mechanism: Presupposition attack. The audience presupposes "I need more
discipline." The hook rejects the premise without arguing it. The reader must
resolve the new causal model.

Use when: The card's diagnosis is that the commonly-assumed cause is wrong.

---

### 2. The Pacing Triple + Lead

Three verifiable truths about the audience's experience, then one reframe.
The agreement momentum from the three truths carries the reader into accepting
the fourth.

> "You've tried the habits. You've built the routines. You've done everything the
> advice said to do. The problem was never the system."

Mechanism: Commitment and consistency. Each "yes" mentally increases investment
in the next line.

Use when: The card's source material describes the failure of conventional
approaches before introducing the real diagnosis.

---

### 3. The False Elimination

Name the two or three things the audience expects to be the answer. Dismiss each
one. Then withhold the actual cause.

> "It's not your discipline. It's not your environment. It's not your routine."

Mechanism: Zeigarnik effect. The brain cannot rest with an open list. The
dismissed items increase urgency for the real answer.

Use when: The card's diagnosis is surprising relative to conventional wisdom.
The false elimination makes the diagnosis feel earned.

---

### 4. The Identity Threat

Frame a common behavior as evidence of a broken self-model, not a missing skill.

> "Every time you look for a better system, you're proving the system isn't the
> problem."

Mechanism: Self-concept maintenance. The reader either rejects the threat
(scrolls, but remembered it) or accepts it and must resolve the implication.
Both outcomes serve the content.

Use when: The card's core diagnosis is about identity rather than behavior.

---

### 5. The Disruptive Reframe

Take a familiar concept and reframe it in unfamiliar terms that occupy cognitive
resources long enough to create openness.

> "Discipline isn't a skill. It's a symptom."

Mechanism: Disruption compliance (Davis & Knowles). The unexpected framing
creates a moment of cognitive opening. The follow-up suggestion lands without
resistance.

Use when: The card's core insight is a re-categorization of something the
audience thought they understood.

---

### 6. The Specificity Anchor

Open with a precise detail — number, timeframe, name, age, dollar amount — that
creates credibility and makes the claim feel real rather than hypothetical.

> "After 600 coaching conversations, one pattern showed up in every person who
> couldn't change."

Mechanism: Anchoring bias + specificity heuristic. Precise numbers are processed
as evidence. They anchor the reader's expectation of what follows. Odd or
non-round numbers feel more credible than round ones.

Use when: The card's source contains observed patterns or data-grounded claims.

---

### 7. The Violated Expectation

State the outcome the audience expects, then contradict it with what actually
happens.

> "The harder you try to change, the more you become what you're trying to change
> from."

Mechanism: Pattern interrupt + information gap. The contradiction forces a
"why?" that only the content can answer.

Use when: The card's mechanism is counterintuitive. Works especially well for
identity-loop content.

---

### 8. The Anti-Vision Frame

Make the reader feel the weight of the future they are currently building, not
the one they want.

> "If nothing changes in the next five years, you already know what Tuesday looks
> like."

Mechanism: Loss aversion. Negative vision is visceral; positive vision is
abstract. The reader is more motivated by the fear of the current trajectory
than by the appeal of the alternative.

Use when: The card's diagnosis involves the reader being stuck in a default life
they have not consciously chosen.

---

### 9. The Paradox Pivot

State two things that seem incompatible. Let the tension sit. Force the reader
to resolve it.

> "The most disciplined people I've seen had no discipline to speak of."

Mechanism: Curiosity peak. The brain cannot file contradictory information
without resolution. It must keep going.

Use when: The card's core insight lives inside a genuine paradox.

---

### 10. The Confirmation + Extension

Validate what the reader already suspects, then extend it into territory they
haven't reached yet.

> "You were right that the problem isn't motivation. Here's the part nobody
> reaches."

Mechanism: Confirmation bias + information gap. The validation creates rapport
and safety. The extension creates a new gap the confirmation alone doesn't close.

Use when: The card's diagnosis builds on a widely-held intuition but takes it
further than conventional discourse.

## Spoken hook mechanics

The spoken hook is written for the ear, not the eye. Test it by reading it
aloud in a single breath.

Rules:
- 10 words or fewer in most cases
- Direct address ("You"), imperative ("Stop"), or declarative ("Discipline is a
  side effect")
- No dependent clauses that require re-reading
- No setup that needs another sentence to land
- Works without context — it must grab a cold audience

High-performing first words for this niche:
- "You" (self-referential processing, immediate stakes)
- "Stop" / "Don't" (pattern interrupt, counter to motivational feeds)
- "The" (creates specificity from nothing: "The real reason")
- Declarative nouns that challenge: "Discipline is..." / "Obsession is..."
- A number that creates instant contrast: "Two kinds of people..."

Avoid:
- "Here's how to..." (instruction framing, not diagnostic)
- "Most people think..." (overused in this space, no longer a pattern interrupt)
- "The truth is..." (has become self-aware cliché in self-help content)
- Questions that can be answered with "no" or "I don't care"

---

## Text hook mechanics

The text hook is written for the eye. It pairs with the spoken hook as the
second layer of the information gap.

Rules:
- 20 words or fewer
- Creates or deepens the open loop from the spoken hook
- Can use ellipsis (…) to signal incompleteness and force a "see more" response
- May use a colon to set up a payoff that arrives later in the content, not in
  the hook itself
- One sentence or two very short sentences
- Must not answer the question the spoken hook opens

The text hook should feel like the spoken hook's shadow: same event, different
angle.

## Power vocabulary for this niche

These words earn attention from this audience without triggering the "generic
self-help" rejection reflex.

**Gap-creating:** the actual, underneath, before that, what's really, the part
nobody, the missing piece, the wrong order, the real cost, already

**Identity-activating:** your model, your operating system, your identity, your
version, the person you're insisting on being

**Diagnostic:** the mechanism, the loop, the pattern, the causal direction, the
layer underneath

**Temporal:** still, yet, already, before, by the time

**Specificity anchors:** every, always, never, the exact, all of it, the whole
thing (use carefully — only when the content supports totalism)

Words to avoid in hooks (overused in this space, no longer trigger attention):
- hustle, grind, discipline (as a positive), consistency, mindset
- "level up" (banned), "unlock" (banned), "game changer" (banned)
- "most people" as a opener (exhausted)
- amazing, incredible, insane (no credibility signal)
- any word that requires the audience to already agree with the framing

## The two-hook pairing test

Before finalizing a hook pair, run this test:

1. Say the spoken hook aloud. Does it land in one breath without context?
2. Read the text hook silently. Does it deepen the gap the spoken hook opened,
   without closing it?
3. Do they work together without saying the same thing?
4. Does either hook close the loop it opened? If yes, it is not a hook — it is a
   thesis statement. Rewrite.
5. Can a cold audience who has never heard of this idea feel the tension
   immediately?

If any answer is wrong, rewrite before proceeding.

## Anti-patterns that kill hooks for this audience

**The advisor frame.** "Here's how to..." starts from the assumption that the
reader needs instruction. This audience has had enough instruction. They need
diagnosis. Never open an angle with advice framing.

**The tease without teeth.** "There's one thing that changes everything" — this
has been so overused that it no longer creates a gap. It signals low-quality
content to a well-read audience.

**Generic self-help inversion.** "Stop grinding, start living" — the inversion
of hustle culture advice is now its own cliché. It signals "I've heard this
before." The hook must go one level deeper than the contrarian position.

**The inspirational close.** Any hook that ends with emotional uplift is a quote
card, not a hook. This voice ends on tension, not resolution.

**Universal claims without grounding.** "Everyone does this" — if the hook makes
a claim that feels too broad to be true, the audience's skepticism fires before
curiosity. Ground the claim: "Every person I've spoken to who felt stuck..."

**Asking questions the audience doesn't care about answering.** If the question
can be dismissed with "no" or "not me," the hook is dead. Use statements that
provoke questions rather than questions that can be deflected.

---

## Hook checklist (run before finalizing any angle)

- Spoken hook works in one breath, cold, without context
- Text hook deepens the gap without closing it
- At least two layers from the trigger stack are present
- No advice framing in either hook
- No banned vocabulary (see Voice DNA)
- No vague qualifiers in generated text
- The gap is calibrated: audience has context to care, not enough to resolve
- Neither hook summarizes or reveals the content's conclusion
- The pair creates tension, not uplift
- The hooks are true to the card's actual diagnosis

## Examples tuned to this voice and niche

**Pattern: Causal Inversion**
- Spoken: "You don't build discipline. Obsession builds it for you."
- Text: "The entire self-help industry taught the causal chain backwards."

**Pattern: False Elimination**
- Spoken: "It's not your habits. It's not your routine. It's not your focus."
- Text: "The thing that's actually keeping you stuck doesn't appear on any
  productivity list."

**Pattern: Identity Threat**
- Spoken: "Every new system you try is proof the system was never the problem."
- Text: "You're not stuck because you haven't found the right method yet."

**Pattern: Anti-Vision Frame**
- Spoken: "Describe your life in five years if nothing changes."
- Text: "You already know what that Tuesday looks like. That's the fuel."

**Pattern: Violated Expectation**
- Spoken: "The people who changed the most never tried to change."
- Text: "They changed something underneath the behavior. The behavior followed."

**Pattern: Paradox Pivot**
- Spoken: "You have to stop trying to get better before you can get better."
- Text: "The effort is the evidence that the wrong thing is being worked on."

## Format selection intelligence (owenmeetsworld)

Each content format has a different relationship with the viewer's attention.
Matching the diagnosis to the right format determines whether the idea lands or
disappears. Use this when assigning `formats` to a card.

---

### green-screen

The creator talks over a visual background. Viewer reads the text while
listening. Works at any attention level — the visual carries the idea even with
sound off.

**Best for:** Idea breakdowns, causal inversions, frameworks, anything that
benefits from a second layer of information running simultaneously.

**Hook implication:** The spoken hook is the primary entry. The visual
reinforces it. Write the spoken hook to work completely standalone — the
visual is support, not the load-bearing element.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** require a small amount of context before
the idea lands, benefit from a visual example or reference, or need the creator's
voice to carry conviction.

---

### yap

Straight-to-camera. No visual overlay. Just the person and what they're saying.

**Best for:** Raw takes, personal observations, short confessions, single-sentence
diagnoses that do not need explanation — they need presence.

**Hook implication:** The spoken hook is everything. There is no safety net. The
first 3 seconds of delivery determine whether anyone stays. Write the spoken
hook as a statement that sounds like it came from lived experience, not from
research.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** are direct, personal, short, and
self-contained. If the idea requires more than 90 seconds, yap is probably
the wrong format.

---

### quick-hits

Clips and text overlay. No face required. The idea is delivered through
on-screen text over relevant footage or b-roll.

**Best for:** Concept distillations, single-mechanism ideas, hook-led content
where the viewer reads before they commit to watching.

**Hook implication:** The text hook becomes the visual anchor. It must land in
under 5 words of on-screen reading. Write the text hook for maximum compression —
the viewer is reading at scroll speed.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** can be stated in a short declarative line,
do not require the creator's face to establish trust, and benefit from visual
proof running alongside the claim.

---

### vlog

Longer format with voiceover and scene cuts. Tells a real story. The viewer
commits more time than any short format.

**Best for:** Narrative arcs, comeback stories, process documentation, the
before/after of a belief change.

**Hook implication:** The spoken hook opens the story, not the idea. The hook
creates narrative tension ("I was wrong about something I'd believed for 5
years") rather than concept tension. The diagnosis arrives later — earned by the
story.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** have a personal story attached, unfold
over time, or require the viewer to feel the problem before they can receive
the reframe.

---

### carousel-dump

Casual photos or screenshots stacked into a post. Low production. Feels like
access rather than content.

**Best for:** Documenting milestones, behind-the-scenes moments, real-life
proof of an idea in action. Not a primary vehicle for complex diagnoses.

**Hook implication:** The first slide is the hook. It should create a reason
to swipe — not explain what follows. Curiosity gap or social proof ("what
happened when I tried this") works better than a thesis statement.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** have a visual proof component, benefit
from "I did this and here's what happened" framing, or are better shown
than argued.

---

### produced-carousel

Designed slides. Template-based or custom. The visual quality signals that the
content is worth reading.

**Best for:** Distilled insights, comparisons, frameworks broken into steps,
opinion pieces that benefit from a scannable structure.

**Hook implication:** Slide 1 is the hook. It must work as a standalone image
that stops the scroll. The text hook from the angle is the first-slide copy.
Write it to work at a glance — 8 words on screen, high contrast, one clear
claim.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** can be broken into a short sequence of
supporting points, benefit from a clean visual hierarchy, or pair well with
a "here are the X things" structure without becoming generic.

---

### text-post

Written post on LinkedIn or X. No visual. The words carry everything.

**Best for:** Opinion-driven takes, diagnostic observations, threads that build
an argument line by line.

**Hook implication:** The first line is the hook. On LinkedIn, it is the line
before "see more." On X, it is the first tweet. It must earn the click or the
scroll in under 10 words. Write it as the sharpest possible statement of the
diagnosis — then let the post unpack it.

**This format suits diagnoses that:** are intellectually surprising, challenge
a widely held belief, or work as a single declarative statement that the post
then proves. Strong for the causal inversion and false elimination patterns.

---

## Format-to-pattern matching

Use this as a quick reference when deciding which angle to lead with for each
format.

| Format | Strongest hook patterns |
|--------|------------------------|
| green-screen | Causal Inversion, Pacing Triple + Lead, Specificity Anchor |
| yap | Identity Threat, Paradox Pivot, Violated Expectation |
| quick-hits | Disruptive Reframe, False Elimination, Anti-Vision Frame |
| vlog | Pacing Triple + Lead, Confirmation + Extension, Specificity Anchor |
| carousel-dump | Anti-Vision Frame, Pacing Triple + Lead |
| produced-carousel | False Elimination, Causal Inversion, Confirmation + Extension |
| text-post | Disruptive Reframe, Identity Threat, Causal Inversion |

The same card can be produced in multiple formats. When it is, write a separate
angle for each format where the hook approach differs. Do not force one angle
to serve formats with incompatible hook mechanics.
